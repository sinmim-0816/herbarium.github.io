Protecting Your Herbarium

Safe Storage Environment

  • Freezing incoming specimens to -29°C for 5 days before entering the herbarium
  • Maintaining a controlled storage environment
  • Careful monitoring for insect activity
  • Providing staff training and information
Handling Guidelines
  • Always keep specimens horizontal and flat—never bend them.
  • Use a sheet of cardboard when carrying specimens.
  • Avoid shuffling specimens to prevent damage to edges.
  • Protect specimens from sunlight, dust, wind, and moisture.
  • Ensure sheets are properly aligned when storing to avoid damage to the edges.

Repair Technique

Specimen Conservation Issues and Solutions
Damaged/Problem Solution
Cellophane Covering
  • Issues: Cellophane can deteriorate quickly, making it hard to get a clear digital image of the specimen.
  • Solution: Replace the cellophane with an archival tissue flap, a protective white paper four-flap folder, or a grey archival four-flap box.
Thin Mounting Paper
  • Issues: Thin mounting paper can break specimens and thin cards may warp.
  • Solution: Attach the sheet to an archival mounting board (42 x 26.5 cm) with water-activated gummed tape. For fragile specimens, use a white archival four-flap folder with a backing board.
Bulky Specimens Damage
  • Issues: Bulky specimens can damage those placed on top, especially if they’re in the plastic bag.
  • Solution: Place the sheet in a lightweight card four-flap folder or box at the bottom of the species folder. Alternatively, put the bulky specimen on top.
Dust or Soot
  • Issues: Dust or soot can hide labels and surface details on a specimen and cause damage over time
  • Solution: Use a smoke sponge or soft brushes to gently clean the dirt without harming the specimen.
Insect Damage to Specimens
  • Issues: Insect damage signs include grazed flowers, droppings, or larvae skins. Herbarium beetles can destroy specimens.
  • Solution: Freeze affected specimens at -30°C for 5 days and record their location. Use a vacuum to remove droppings and damaged material. Repair badly affected specimens with stitching or tape, and protect very fragile specimens with a white archival four-flap folder and backing board.
