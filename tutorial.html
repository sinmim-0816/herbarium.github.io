Fresh Leaves to Perfect Specimens

Step-by-Step Guide



What is Herbarium?

A herbarium is a specialized collection of pressed and dried plant specimens used for research in biodiversity, ecology, and evolution. Like a library, it stores plant information rather than books, with specimens organized for easy access and preservation. Herbaria, such as the Duke Herbarium (DUKE), gather samples from various locations and time periods, aiding in the study of plant diversity, ecological patterns, and evolutionary changes.


Herbarium
A collection of dried and preserved plant specimens used for scientific research, documenting plant diversity, and studying ecological and evolutionary patterns.
DUKE
The Duke Herbarium, a research facility at Duke University that collects, preserves, and catalogs plant specimens through fieldwork and exchanges, contributing to global plant science research.
Specimen
A sample of a plant that has been pressed, dried, and labeled for scientific study and preservation.

History of Herbarium

Herbaria began with Luca Ghini in the 16th century, who glued plant specimens onto sheets of paper, bound them into volumes, and stored them like books. In 1751, Carolus Linnaeus revolutionized this method by advising the use of single sheets for each specimen, which could be stored in a flexible cabinet. This innovation allowed for easy insertion, removal, and rearrangement of specimens, accommodating new material and reflecting updated knowledge.



The Craftsmanship Behind Specimen Perfection

  • Step 1: Collecting

    Where to Collect

    • Best Spots: Edges of pathways, field and woodland borders, fence lines, and railways.
    • Avoid: Private property, nature preserves, and protected species. Use caution near roadways.

    What to Collect

    • Optimal Timing: Collect plants when they are flowering for best results.
    • Specimen Quality: Choose healthy, pest-free, and dry plants. Collect all parts that aid in identification, and note characteristics like smell, texture, and height. Record the date of collection.

  • Step 2: Preparation

    Protecting Specimens

    • Transport: Use sealable plastic bags and a sturdy bag to protect plants from heat and damage.
    • Immediate Pressing: Press plants as soon as possible. If not pressing right away, keep in the refrigerator for up to a day.

    Arranging Specimens:

    • Materials: Use thin plywood and parchment paper for pressing.
    • Method: Place plants with similar moisture content together. Use parchment paper and tape to arrange plants carefully, avoiding overlap.

  • Step 3: Pressing

    Pressing Process:

    • Setup: Load plants between layers of parchment in the press. Apply pressure using a plant press or heavy books.
    • Monitoring: Check after 24 hours. Replace wrinkled parchment and adjust pressure if needed. Drying takes about a week, up to two weeks for flesh plants.

  • Step 4: Mounting

    Mounting Specimens:

    • Materials: Use acid-free paper and acrylic adhesive.
    • Process: Apply adhesive to the plant, avoiding direct contact with skin. Use tweezers and wooden skewers to position the plant. Ensure all parts are adhered.

  • Step 5: Freezing

    Freezing Process:

    • Setup: Stack mounted specimens between parchment layers, wrapped in plastic and strapped tightly. Freeze for 72 hours to eliminate pests.

  • Step 6: Identification

    Identifying Plants:

    • Initial Identification: Record fresh plant details like color and scent. Use a dichotomous key and hand lens for accuracy.
    • Verification: Compare specimens with photographs and botanical illustrations for confirmation.

  • Step 7: Cataloging

    Preserving Specimens:

    • Cataloging: Assign a number and link it to a digital database with plant information.
    • Storage: Place specimens in transparent, acid-free sleeves, and store in wooden cases with cedar shavings. Inspect regularly for insect damage.

Creating an Herbarium Label

Labels are crucial to the value of a herbarium specimen, as inaccurate or missing labels render it scientifically and historically useless.

Key pieces of information that must be included:

  • Scientific name (including family, genus, and species)
  • Collection date
  • The locality
  • Specimen number or collection number
  • Collector's Name
  • Habitat Notes

Things need to take note:

  • Include a label with each specimen in standard size newsprint/paper ( 11cm x 7cm )
  • Do not staple labels to newsprint
  • Do not tape labels to newsprint
  • Do not paperclip labels to newsprint
